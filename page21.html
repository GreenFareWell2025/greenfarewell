Description

Hibiscus plants are shrubs or small trees that grow up to 15 feet tall. They have shiny, dark green leaves and large, showy flowers in various colors, such as red, pink, orange, yellow, and white.


The flowers are five-petaled and funnel-shaped, with a prominent stigma and style in the center.


  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Family: Malvaceae
  • Genus: Hibiscus
  • Species: Over 200 species, including Hibiscus rosa-sinensis, Hibiscus sabdariffa, and Hibiscus syriacus.

Origin and Habitat

  • Native to warm and tropical regions, including Asia, Africa, and the Pacific Islands.
  • Hibiscus plants thrive in sunny climates with moist, well-drained soil and moderate rainfall. They are often found in gardens, parks, and along roadsides.
  • In Hawaii, the hibiscus flower is a symbol of hospitality and is often worn as a decoration.
  • It is the national flower of Malaysia and is featured prominently in cultural and artistic expressions.

Types of Hibiscus

  • Chinese Hibiscus (Hibiscus rosa-sinensis)**: The most common variety used for ornamental purposes.
  • Roselle (Hibiscus sabdariffa)**: Known for its edible calyx, often used to make herbal tea.
  • Rose of Sharon (Hibiscus syriacus)**: A deciduous species popular in temperate regions.

Medicinal Properties

  • Heart Health: Hibiscus tea helps lower blood pressure and cholesterol levels.
  • Digestive Aid: It supports digestion and alleviates constipation.
  • Skin Care: The flower's extracts are used in cosmetics for skin hydration and anti-aging.
  • Anti-inflammatory: It can help reduce inflammation in the body
  • Weight Management: Drinking hibiscus tea may assist in weight control.

Uses

  • Culinary: Hibiscus is used to make jams, jellies, syrups, and teas. Roselle calyces are a key ingredient in beverages worldwide.
  • Cosmetics: Extracts from hibiscus are included in shampoos, conditioners, and skin creams.
  • Landscaping: Its vibrant blooms make it a popular choice for gardens and hedges.

Fun Facts

  • Hibiscus flowers are edible and often used as garnishes in salads and desserts.
  • The plant is also known as the "shoe flower" because its petals were historically used to polish shoes.
